SBIR Phase I Overview

PromptGenix LLC · Women & Minority-Owned · Contact: dohoon.kim1@icloud.com
The problem

Biomedical data generation outpaces analysis and interpretation.

NIH-funded laboratories routinely generate RNA-Seq, scRNA-Seq, and high-dimensional flow cytometry data, yet analysis remains fragmented across tools and scripts. Turnaround is slow, reproducibility is inconsistent, and scientific interpretation depends on limited expert bandwidth.

The innovation

PromptGenix: an automated BioAI co-investigator.

PromptGenix integrates reproducible analysis pipelines with a literature-aware AI layer that produces human-readable summaries, ranked biological hypotheses, and concrete follow-up experiments. The platform transforms raw FASTQ and FCS files into actionable, reviewer-ready outputs.

Technical innovation

  • End-to-end automation: RNA-Seq and flow cytometry pipelines with standardized outputs
  • Reproducibility: deterministic configurations and versioned reports
  • BioAI layer: hypothesis generation grounded in observed signals and PubMed evidence
  • Deployment flexibility: local-first execution with optional secure cloud scaling

SBIR Phase I focus

  • Harden pipelines on multiple public datasets (GEO, SRA, FlowRepository)
  • Validate hypothesis generator traceability and usefulness
  • Measure reductions in turnaround time and improvements in reproducibility
  • Produce reviewer-ready HTML/PDF reports
Boundary: Phase I validation uses public data only to avoid IP and data-use constraints.
Why it matters

Faster, more interpretable science with lower operational burden.

By automating analysis and embedding scientific reasoning directly into the workflow, PromptGenix enables small and mid-sized labs to operate with PI-level analytical capacity. The result is faster insight generation, improved rigor, and clearer decision-making for experimental design.

Commercial relevance: the same capabilities validated in Phase I directly support a scalable commercialization path to academic labs, CROs, and government research groups in Phase II.
