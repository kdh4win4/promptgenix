Research Strategy – Approach

Phase I integrates hardened automated pipelines for RNA-Seq and flow cytometry with a literature-aware LLM layer that produces traceable interpretations and a Hypothesis Generator (ranked hypotheses + concrete validation experiments). Validation uses public datasets only to avoid IP/data-use conflicts and to ensure reproducibility.

Outputs: Versioned configs · Reproducible reports (HTML/PDF) · Figures · Methods · PubMed-linked evidence.

A. Pipeline hardening

Make RNA-Seq and flow cytometry workflows robust, repeatable, and reviewer-traceable.

  • RNA-Seq: QC → alignment/quantification → differential expression → GSEA/GSVA, with versioned configs and deterministic outputs.
  • Flow cytometry: preprocessing/QC, transforms, batch normalization, and summary exports (for downstream statistics + reporting).
  • Reproducibility: run manifests, environment/version stamping, and standardized report structure for easy audit.
Phase I constraint: public datasets only (GEO/SRA/FlowRepository), enabling conflict-free validation.
B. Literature-aware hypothesis generator

Generate ranked hypotheses and concrete follow-up experiments with PubMed traceability.

  • Evidence linkage: connect literature mechanisms to observed signals (DEG, pathways, immune subsets).
  • Hypothesis ranking: score by strength/consistency of evidence + coherence with the dataset.
  • Experiment suggestions: propose actionable validation steps (panel refinements, contrasts, perturbations) with citations.
Deliverable: a structured “Hypothesis Table” in the report with citations and proposed tests.
C. Phase I Validation Plan

Public-data validation with measurable KPIs and reviewer-ready outputs.

Phase I evaluation focuses on pipeline stability, reproducibility, speed, and user-rated usefulness of AI-generated hypotheses. Datasets are selected to represent typical NIH lab workflows and common failure modes (batch effects, heterogeneous cohorts, noisy markers).

Risks & Mitigations

Phase I technical risks are known and manageable with clear mitigations.

Phase I focuses on hardening, traceability, and public-data validation. The table below summarizes anticipated risks and concrete mitigations that keep deliverables on schedule.

Risk Why it matters Mitigation (Phase I) Go / No-Go criteria
Public dataset heterogeneity
formats, metadata gaps		 Inconsistent inputs can break automation and reduce reproducibility. Standard ingestion templates; robust parsers; minimum metadata requirements; fail-fast checks with actionable error messages. ≥3 datasets processed end-to-end without manual code edits; documented input schema.
Batch effects confound signals
flow and RNA-Seq		 False positives/negatives degrade trust and usability. Batch-aware normalization options; pre/post batch diagnostics in the report; transparent methods and limitations. Demonstrable reduction in batch-driven variance; stable results across reruns.
LLM hallucination / unverifiable claims
scientific narrative risk		 Untraceable statements are unacceptable for reviewers and PIs. Structured templates; citations required for mechanistic claims; “evidence level” labels; uncertainty + “what would falsify this” prompts. All hypotheses include citations or are explicitly marked speculative with rationale.
Hypotheses not actionable
too generic / not testable		 Value depends on producing experiments a lab can realistically run. Enforce testability fields (assay/panel/contrast); rank by actionability; complexity tags; pilot feedback loop. ≥80% pilot-rated “plausible & actionable” on a structured rubric.
Compute/time constraints
local vs cloud		 Long runtimes reduce adoption and Phase I throughput. Modular execution; caching intermediates; local or AWS VPC execution; prioritize “good defaults” over exhaustive search in Phase I. Reviewer-ready report produced in <24 hours from raw inputs on target hardware profile.
Phase I readiness: Mitigations are implemented as product features (templates, constraints, traceability, reporting), not manual reviewer-dependent steps.
